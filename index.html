As a longtime anime enthusiast, I couldn't help but notice my growing disinterest in popular titles. I realized a big reason was because I noticed a lack of female representation and main characters. That observation sent me down a data rabbit hole:

Can I quantify just how underrepresented women are in anime?

Using data from MyAnimeList's top 2,000 anime by popularity, I scraped character biographies, extracted gender, personality traits, and roles for over 12,000 characters, and built this interactive analysis to find out!

Setting The Landscape: Popularity, Rating, and Gender

The most popular and highest-rated anime cluster in the upper right, and when colored by female representation, that corner goes very teal. When you filter to anime with ≥50% female main characters, you can really see how the top-right thins out.

Higher-Rated Anime Have Fewer Female Main Characters

Across genres, female main characters are more prominent in genres like romance and fantasy, but fall behind in more action and fighting-based genres. Additionally, when sorted by score, a clear pattern emerges: the highest-rated anime (8.5+) are significantly more male-dominated than lower-rated titles.

In Top-Rated Anime, Female Characters Are Pushed to the Sidelines

Even among female characters that do appear in highly-rated anime, the majority are supporting rather than main characters. In the 8.5+ score tier, the gap between female main and supporting characters is 14% higher than the equivalent gap for male characters.

Male Characters Are Defined by Competence, while Female Characters by Appearance & Warmth.

The most common traits for male characters (strong, skilled, powerful, serious) focus on their abilities and agency. However, female characters are most often described as beautiful, shy, or cheerful: traits that define how they look or how they make others feel, not what they are capable of achieving.

...But Has Representation Been Improving?

The % of female MCs rose steadily through the 2000s, peaking around 2009–2013. But since 2014, that % has dropped back below 50% and shows no sign of recovering thus far...
